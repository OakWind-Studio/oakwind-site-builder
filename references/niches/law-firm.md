# Niche: Boutique Law Firm (3-15 Attorneys)

Covers boutique and mid-size law firms with 3-15 attorneys specializing in personal injury, family law, criminal defense, or multi-practice areas. NOT solo practitioners (see `niche-attorney.md` for solo). These are established firms with named partners, associate attorneys, support staff, and often multiple offices. The firm brand matters as much as individual attorney brands.

Boutique law firms are one of the highest-value niches for OakWind. These firms spend aggressively on marketing ($10K-$50K/month on paid ads alone) and understand that a premium website is a competitive weapon, not a digital brochure. A $5K-$10K website is a rounding error on their annual marketing budget — but a bad website undermines every dollar they spend driving traffic to it.

---

## Why This Niche Pays Premium

- **Marketing spend context:** Firms spending $10K-$50K/mo on Google Ads need a website that converts that traffic. A $5K-$10K site that increases conversion by even 5% pays for itself in weeks.
- **Case values justify everything:** A single personal injury case can settle for $500K-$5M. One family law retainer runs $10K-$25K. The website cost is invisible next to case revenue.
- **Competitive landscape is brutal:** In any metro area, 20+ firms are bidding on the same "car accident lawyer [city]" keywords. The website is what separates a click from a signed retainer.
- **The website IS the pitch deck:** Potential clients comparison-shop 3-5 firm websites before calling. The firm that looks most established, credible, and results-driven wins the call.
- **Referral source validation:** Other attorneys and professionals check the website before referring clients. A cheap-looking site kills referral flow.
- **Multi-attorney complexity:** Team pages, individual bios, practice area cross-linking, and multi-location support all push toward Tier 3-4 pricing ($5K-$10K).

## Required Page Elements

- **Firm hero with firm name, founding year, and primary CTA** — The firm brand leads, not an individual attorney. Firm name in large, authoritative display type. Founding year or "Established [year]" signals longevity.
- **Attorney team grid with individual bios** — Every attorney gets a card in the grid. Clicking/hovering reveals credentials, practice focus, bar admissions, and education. Named partners get larger cards or a featured section above associates.
- **Practice area pages with detailed descriptions** — Not a bulleted list. Each practice area needs a dedicated section or page with: what the firm handles, the firm's approach, relevant case results, and a CTA. Minimum 3-4 paragraphs per area.
- **Case results with mandatory disclaimers** — Settlement amounts, verdict amounts, or case descriptions organized by practice area. Every results section MUST include: "Past results do not guarantee future outcomes" or equivalent state-specific disclaimer.
- **Firm story / heritage section** — When the firm was founded, by whom, why, and what has driven it for [X] years. This is NOT an attorney bio — it's the firm's narrative arc. Heritage imagery, founding partner quotes, community roots.
- **Awards wall** — Super Lawyers, Best Lawyers in America, AV Preeminent, National Trial Lawyers Top 100, state-specific awards. Logo grid or badge display. These are conversion signals, not vanity.
- **Community involvement section** — Pro bono work, sponsorships, local boards, charitable partnerships. Humanizes the firm and signals local investment.
- **Multi-location display** (if applicable) — Office cards with address, phone, hours, and embedded map. Each location should feel like a real office, not a virtual mailbox.
- **"Request a Confidential Consultation" CTA** — Never "Get a Free Quote" or "Contact Us." The word "confidential" signals attorney-client privilege awareness. "Free case evaluation" is acceptable for PI firms.
- **Phone number prominent and click-to-call** — These firms have intake staff. The phone number should be in the nav, hero, and footer. Consider a sticky mobile CTA bar.
- **Client testimonials** (where state bar rules permit) — Outcome-focused, attributed when possible. "The team at [Firm] recovered $1.2M for our family after a devastating accident."

## Trust Signals (priority order)

1. Combined years of experience across the firm ("75+ years of combined legal experience")
2. Case results — total recovered, notable verdicts/settlements
3. Named partner credentials and bar admissions
4. Awards and recognitions — Super Lawyers, Best Lawyers, AV-rated, NTL Top 100
5. Board certifications or specializations
6. Number of attorneys and support staff (signals resources and capacity)
7. Firm founding year / years in operation
8. Community involvement and pro bono record
9. University and law school credentials of the team
10. Published articles, media appearances, speaking engagements

## Section Priority Order

1. Hero (firm name, founding year/tagline, primary practice focus, CTA)
2. Trust strip (years of practice, total recovered, cases handled, attorneys on staff)
3. Practice areas (organized by specialty with brief descriptions and links)
4. Attorney team (partner-featured grid with associate cards below)
5. Case results (stats-forward with category organization)
6. Firm story / heritage (why the firm exists, founding narrative)
7. Awards wall (logo grid of recognitions and badges)
8. Testimonials (outcome-focused, practice-area relevant)
9. Community involvement
10. Contact / consultation request (multi-location if applicable)
11. Footer (all locations, practice areas, bar disclaimer)

## Aesthetic Direction

Boutique law firm websites must project **institutional authority, deep competence, and premium positioning**. Think top-tier management consulting firm (McKinsey, Bain) or a private wealth advisory — not a dusty courthouse office. The design must communicate that this firm has resources, depth, and a track record that solo practitioners cannot match.

**Light mode is the default.** White or warm-white base with strategically placed dark sections (navy, charcoal) that create visual gravitas without oppressive heaviness. The dark sections house case results, the firm story, or awards — elements that benefit from the "stage" effect of a dark background.

### Color Approach

- **Light primary backgrounds** with generous white space — signals confidence and institutional quality
- **Dark accent sections** (deep navy, charcoal, or near-black) for key moments: case results, firm heritage, awards wall. These create rhythm and dramatic emphasis without making the entire site feel heavy.
- Single refined accent color: gold, burgundy, or deep blue — used sparingly for CTAs, borders, and highlights
- Avoid: bright colors, gradients, anything that reads as "marketing agency" rather than "law firm"
- Avoid: pure black backgrounds — always warm charcoal or deep navy for dark sections

### Typography

- **Firm name as the dominant typographic element** — larger than any headline, treated with reverence. Consider all-caps with generous letter-spacing.
- Elegant serif for headlines — signals tradition, gravitas, and institutional permanence
- Clean sans-serif for body and navigation — modern professionalism
- Restrained sizing hierarchy: the firm name is huge, section headers are confident but not competing, body text is readable
- Never: script fonts, handwritten styles, overly trendy display fonts, or anything that could appear in a tech startup

### Photography

- **Team photos:** Professional, well-lit, consistent background and styling across all attorneys. Ideally shot in the same session for visual cohesion. Business attire is mandatory.
- **Office interiors:** Modern conference rooms, law libraries, lobbies. These signal real physical presence and resources.
- **Courthouse/city imagery:** The local courthouse, city skyline, or recognizable local landmarks ground the firm in its community.

## Sub-Niche Variations

### Personal Injury Firm

- **Highest marketing spend** in legal — $20K-$50K/mo on ads is common. The website MUST convert.
- Results-forward design: total recovered in oversized type ("$250M+ Recovered For Our Clients"), individual case results below.
- More aggressive, emotionally driven CTAs: "Injured? We Fight For You." / "No Fee Unless We Win."
- Emergency/urgency messaging: "Available 24/7 — Free Case Evaluation"
- Warmer, more empathetic tone than other legal sub-niches — clients are injured people, not businesses
- Client video testimonials carry enormous weight if the firm has them
- Spanish-language toggle or bilingual elements are common and valuable
- Hero should convey both empathy AND strength — "We care about you AND we will destroy the insurance company"

### Family Law Firm

- **Warmer aesthetic** — earth tones and muted palettes are appropriate. Deep green, warm taupe, soft burgundy.
- Sensitivity in messaging: "Protecting Your Family's Future" / "Compassionate, Aggressive Representation"
- Privacy emphasis: "Confidential consultations in a private setting"
- Process-focused sections: "What to Expect in a Divorce" or "Your Custody Case Timeline"
- Avoid combative language — family law clients are emotionally fragile. Words like "fight" and "battle" should be balanced with "protect," "guide," and "advocate."
- Children and family imagery (tasteful, not stock-photo) grounds the practice in human stakes
- Attorney bios should emphasize training in mediation, collaborative law, or negotiation — not just trial wins

### Criminal Defense Firm

- **Darkest, most serious palette** of all sub-niches — deep navy, charcoal, black. Authority and gravitas maxed out.
- Urgency-driven: "Arrested? Call Now — 24/7 Defense Hotline"
- Confidentiality as a core message: "Everything You Tell Us Is Protected By Attorney-Client Privilege"
- Court experience stats: trials won, cases dismissed, charges reduced
- Former prosecutor backgrounds are gold — "Former [County] District Attorney" is a headline-level credential
- Avoid: anything that feels judgmental. The tone should be: "We don't judge. We defend."
- Rights-focused copy: "Your freedom is at stake. Your rights matter."
- Bail bond and emergency consultation accessibility should be prominent

### Multi-Practice Firm

- **Most complex build** — requires clear information architecture so visitors can self-select their practice area quickly
- Practice area mega-menu or prominent practice area grid immediately after the hero
- Each practice area should feel like a mini-site within the larger firm site
- Firm heritage and depth are the differentiators: "One firm. Every legal need."
- Attorney profiles should cross-reference which practice areas each attorney serves
- Risk of feeling generic — the design must signal specialization depth, not "we do everything" dilution
- Consider tabbed or accordion sections for practice areas to manage page length

## Attorney Team Display Patterns

### Partner-Featured + Associate Grid
The default pattern for most boutique firms. Named partners get large cards (photo, name, title, practice focus, and a brief quote or philosophy statement) displayed prominently at the top. Below, associates and of-counsel attorneys appear in a tighter grid with smaller cards. Clicking any card opens a modal or navigates to an individual bio page with full credentials, bar admissions, education, and notable cases.

### Grid with Hover Bios
All attorneys displayed in a uniform grid (3-4 columns on desktop). On hover, the card expands or overlays with: practice area focus, years of experience, and a one-line credential highlight. Click for full bio. Best for firms where all attorneys are positioned equally (no "star partner" dynamic).

### Carousel with Featured Attorney
A horizontal carousel that spotlights one attorney at a time with a large photo, detailed bio, and key credentials. Navigation arrows or auto-advance cycle through the team. Best for firms with 5+ attorneys where a grid would feel overwhelming. Include a "View All Attorneys" link below.

### Bio Page Structure (for individual attorney pages)
If the build includes individual attorney pages, each should contain:
- Professional headshot (large, high quality)
- Full name with title (Partner, Associate, Of Counsel)
- Practice areas served
- Bar admissions (state, federal, supreme court)
- Education (law school, undergraduate, honors)
- Notable case results or representative matters
- Professional memberships and awards
- Published articles or speaking engagements
- Personal paragraph (community ties, hobbies — humanizes the attorney)
- Direct contact info and "Schedule a Consultation" CTA

## Case Results Display Patterns

### Stats-Forward Hero Block
A dark-background section with oversized numbers: "$250M+ Recovered" / "5,000+ Cases Handled" / "98% Success Rate" in large display type. Below, a grid of individual case results by category. This pattern leads with the aggregate impact before drilling into specifics.

### Category-Organized Results Table
Case results grouped by practice area (Motor Vehicle Accidents, Workplace Injuries, Medical Malpractice, etc.) with columns for case type, result amount, and brief description. Clean, scannable, data-forward. Each category gets its own card or accordion section.

### Featured Verdicts + Results Grid
2-3 "marquee" results highlighted in large cards at the top (the $5M verdict, the landmark case), followed by a denser grid of additional results below. Creates hierarchy — the biggest wins get the spotlight, but the volume of results below signals consistent performance.

### Mandatory Disclaimer Placement
Every case results section MUST include a visible disclaimer. Place it:
- Directly below the section header, before any results are shown
- In a smaller but still readable font size (not hidden in footer-sized text)
- Standard language: "Past results do not guarantee future outcomes. Each case is unique and must be evaluated on its own merits."
- Some states require additional language — flag for client review

## Copy Patterns That Work

- "Decades of experience. Hundreds of millions recovered."
- "[Firm Name] has fought for [City] families since [Year]."
- "[X] attorneys. One mission: your result."
- "When the stakes are highest, [City] trusts [Firm Name]."
- "Board-certified trial lawyers with a record of results."
- "Confidential consultations — call today or request one online."
- "We built this firm on the principle that every client deserves a team, not just a lawyer."
- "From consultation to courtroom, our team is with you at every step."
- "Exposed. Results in the millions. [Firm Name] delivers."
- "Recognized by Super Lawyers, Best Lawyers, and the National Trial Lawyers — [X] years running."

## Anti-Patterns

- **Treating it like a solo attorney site** — a firm website must showcase the TEAM, not a single personality. The firm brand is primary.
- **Gavel and scales of justice imagery** — the most overused legal cliche in existence. Signals a template site.
- **Generic "Contact Us" CTA** — "Request a Confidential Consultation" or "Free Case Evaluation" converts better and signals professionalism.
- **Hiding attorneys behind stock photos** — real headshots of the actual team are non-negotiable for credibility.
- **Inconsistent attorney photos** — if one partner has a professional headshot and another has a selfie, it signals disorganization. Better to use consistent placeholder styling than mismatched real photos.
- **Case results without disclaimers** — an ethics violation that could get the firm in trouble. Never ship without the disclaimer.
- **"We handle all types of law"** — even multi-practice firms should present organized specialties, not a generic claim of doing everything.
- **Tiny, buried phone number** — for a firm spending $30K/mo on ads, every missed call is hundreds or thousands in lost revenue. Phone number in the nav, hero, and sticky mobile bar.
- **Dark mode as default** — law firm sites should be light-mode primary with dark accent sections. Full dark mode feels too moody and less trustworthy for mainstream legal clients.
- **Playful fonts, animations, or color choices** — nothing about hiring a lawyer is fun. The site should feel serious, competent, and reassuring.
- **One-page scrolling site** — a firm with 3-15 attorneys and multiple practice areas needs proper page structure, not everything crammed onto one scroll.

## ABA Compliance Notes

- **ABA Model Rule 7.1 (Communications Concerning a Lawyer's Services):** All communications, including website content, must not be false or misleading. This includes omitting material facts, creating unjustified expectations, or implying results that cannot be substantiated.
- **ABA Model Rule 7.2 (Communications Concerning a Lawyer's Services: Specific Rules):** Governs advertising and solicitation. Websites are considered advertising in most jurisdictions. Firm must retain copies of website content.
- **Case results disclaimers are mandatory** in virtually all jurisdictions. Standard: "Past results do not guarantee future outcomes." Some states (e.g., New York, Florida) have specific additional requirements.
- **Testimonials:** Some states restrict or prohibit client testimonials entirely. Others allow them with disclaimers. Always flag testimonial sections for client/state bar review before publishing.
- **"Specialist" and "expert" claims:** In most states, an attorney cannot claim to be a "specialist" or "expert" unless board-certified by the relevant state board or an ABA-accredited organization. Use "focused on" or "concentrating in" as safer alternatives.
- **"Free consultation" language:** Generally permitted but check state-specific rules. Some states require disclosure if the consultation may convert to a paid engagement.
- **No language creating attorney-client relationships:** The website should not use "your lawyer," "your attorney," or language implying representation before engagement. Use: "our attorneys," "the firm's team," or "contact us to discuss your case."
- **Multi-state practice:** If the firm is licensed in multiple states, the website should identify which attorneys are admitted in which jurisdictions. A footer disclaimer such as "Licensed in [State(s)]" is standard practice.
- **Lawyer referral disclaimers:** If the firm participates in referral networks, applicable disclosures may be required.
- **Firm name rules:** Some states require that firm names include only the names of current or deceased partners. Fictitious or trade names may require registration. Flag any non-standard firm naming for compliance review.

## Same-Niche Rotation System

Build 10 law firm sites and they should all look different. Pick the variant that matches the firm personality, and rotate so consecutive same-niche builds use different variants.

| | **Variant A: Dark Authoritative / Mahogany** | **Variant B: Classic Navy / Institutional** | **Variant C: Modern Glass / Progressive** |
|---|---|---|---|
| Hero | Dark warm charcoal hero with oversized firm name in all-caps serif and gold founding year badge, dramatic courthouse or skyline silhouette, deep burgundy accent, maximum gravitas | Navy and white split — deep navy hero section with gold firm name and partner names, clean white section with attorney headshot grid preview, institutional authority | Clean bright hero with bold oversized firm name in modern serif, minimal partner headshot strip, animated trust stats (years, recovered, cases), progressive and approachable |
| Palette | Palette C (Warm Prestige — warm cream + deep warm brown + burgundy + muted gold) with dark hero sections, or Obsidian adapted warm | Palette A (Navy Authority — white + deep navy + confident blue + gold) or Navy Brass from color-palettes.md for dark sections | Palette B (Modern Charcoal — warm white + charcoal + electric blue) or Midnight Sage from color-palettes.md |
| Texture | Dark noise grain on hero and case results sections, thin gold horizontal rules, burgundy accent borders, dramatic alternation between dark mahogany sections and warm cream | Navy dark sections alternate with clean white, gold border accents on attorney cards and awards, subtle paper texture on light sections, institutional rhythm | None — ultra-clean with strong typographic hierarchy, sharp geometric grid, thin charcoal lines, precision whitespace |
| Cards | Dark burgundy-tinted cards for practice areas with gold serif headings, attorney cards with warm dark overlay on hover revealing credentials | Navy-topped white cards with gold accent stripe for practice areas, uniform attorney grid with consistent professional headshot treatment | Flat white cards with bold blue left border for practice areas, clean attorney cards with modern hover expand showing credentials |
| CTA style | Burgundy button with gold text on dark sections, gold button with dark text on light sections ("Request a Confidential Consultation") | Navy button with gold hover state, institutional copy ("Free Case Evaluation — Call Today") | Clean blue button with minimal styling, direct progressive copy ("Schedule a Consultation") |
| Special | Dramatic oversized case results on dark background ($250M+ in gold display type), animated typewriter-style founding story section, dark full-width awards wall | Navy awards badge wall with gold-framed logos, partner-featured attorney section with large headshots and credential blocks, formal tabbed practice areas | Interactive practice area mega-grid with clean filtering, animated case results counter with modern number animation, team carousel with individual bio expand |
| Font mood | Heavy prestige serif on dark (Bodoni Moda or Noto Serif Display + DM Sans) | Classic institutional serif (Playfair Display or Cormorant Garamond + Source Sans 3) | Modern progressive serif + clean sans (DM Serif Display or Libre Baskerville + Outfit) |

**How to pick:** Match variant to firm personality. An established trial firm or criminal defense firm wanting maximum authority and courtroom gravitas → Variant A. A traditional multi-practice firm with heritage, named partners, and institutional identity → Variant B. A newer or progressive firm that wants to feel modern, accessible, and differentiated from old-school competitors → Variant C. Document which variant in App.jsx header comment.
