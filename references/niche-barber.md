# Niche: Barbershop

Covers barbershops, men's grooming, fade shops, traditional barbers, and unisex hair studios.

---

## Required Page Elements

- **Services with prices** — the #1 reason someone visits a barbershop website (fades, lineups, beard trims, hot towel shaves, kids cuts)
- **Walk-in vs. appointment messaging** — make this immediately clear
- **Gallery of cuts** — Instagram-style grid showing the barber's actual work
- **Phone number** — click-to-call, sticky, prominent
- **Google rating and review count** — first viewport
- **Hours** — barbers often have non-standard hours, make them easy to find
- **Location with map** — neighborhood matters for barbershops
- **Booking link** (if they use Square, Booksy, or similar)

## Trust Signals (priority order)

1. Gallery of actual cuts (the portfolio IS the pitch)
2. Google rating + review count
3. Years cutting / "Since XXXX"
4. Owner/barber name and photo — personal brand is everything
5. Walk-in friendly / "No appointment needed"
6. Licensed barber credentials
7. Specific neighborhood identity
8. Social media presence (Instagram handle)

## Section Priority Order

1. Hero (barber's best work or shop interior + name + vibe statement)
2. Trust strip (rating, years, "walk-ins welcome")
3. Services + prices (clean, scannable list)
4. Gallery (6-12 photos of cuts, fades, beard work)
5. About (the barber's story, personality, craft)
6. Reviews (specific to haircut quality and experience)
7. Location + hours + booking
8. Footer

## Aesthetic Direction

Barbershops are personal brands. The website should feel like walking into the shop — the owner's personality, the neighborhood vibe, the culture. Design should feel **bold, masculine (or gender-appropriate if unisex), and personality-driven**.

### Color Approach
- Dark themes work well (black, charcoal, deep green, navy)
- Gold or copper accents signal premium/classic
- Red and white for traditional barbershop aesthetic
- Warm wood tones for vintage/classic shops
- Avoid: pastel, corporate blue, overly clinical

### Typography
- Condensed or bold sans-serif for that strong, direct feel
- Slab serifs for vintage/classic barbershops
- Headlines should feel confident and punchy
- Avoid: delicate serifs, script fonts, anything "soft"

### Photography
- Close-up fade photos (the before/after of this niche)
- Chair-level action shots
- Shop interior showing the vibe (vintage chairs, neon, art)
- Owner/barber portrait — personality front and center
- Avoid: stock photos, generic salon imagery

## Copy Patterns That Work

- "Where your style comes to life"
- "Fort Worth's favorite fade shop"
- "Walk-ins always welcome — no appointment needed"
- "[Owner] has been perfecting cuts for [X] years"
- "More than a haircut — it's an experience"
- "The shop where everybody knows your name"
- "Clean cuts. Good vibes. Every time."

## Anti-Patterns

- Generic salon imagery — barbershops have a distinct culture, don't make it look like Supercuts
- No gallery — the cuts ARE the marketing. Without them the site has no proof
- Hiding prices — barbershop customers expect transparent, simple pricing
- Over-designed — barbershops are approachable. Don't make the site feel like a luxury fashion brand unless that's the actual vibe
- No personality — the barber IS the brand. Feature them prominently
